## The Handle Market Is Full of Scams

The secondary market for Instagram handles has no formal regulation, no buyer protection, and no recourse when things go wrong. Scams are common enough that most people with more than a year in this space have either been scammed directly or know someone who has. Here are the seven patterns we see most often.

## Red Flag #1: No Broker, No Call, Just "Send First"

The most basic and most common scam. A seller contacts you (often via Instagram DM or Twitter) claiming to have a valuable handle. When you express interest, they ask you to send payment first and they'll transfer "immediately after." There is no broker, no escrow, no live call — just a promise and a UPI QR code.

If anyone asks you to pay before seeing the transfer happen on a live call, stop the conversation immediately.

## Red Flag #2: Urgency Tactics

"I have another buyer ready to close today." "This price is only valid for 2 hours." "If you don't pay now I'm selling to someone else."

Legitimate sellers do not manufacture urgency. High-value assets go through proper broker processes that take hours or days, not minutes. Artificial urgency is designed to pressure you into skipping due diligence.

## Red Flag #3: The "I'll Do the Transfer for You" Scam

The seller offers to log into your Instagram account and do the transfer themselves — "it's faster that way." This is an account takeover attempt. Never give anyone your Instagram credentials, one-time passwords, or login verification codes for any reason.

A legitimate transfer does not require the buyer's login credentials at any point. You claim the handle yourself on your own device while watching.

## Red Flag #4: Fake Escrow Services

The scammer introduces a "trusted escrow service" (often a website that looks professional but was built last week) to handle the payment. You send funds to the "escrow" which is actually the scammer's second account. The transfer never happens and the escrow "support team" becomes unreachable.

Always verify any escrow service independently. Better yet, use a broker whose business identity is verifiable — name, location, and contact details should be public record.

## Red Flag #5: The Account Doesn't Match What You're Buying

You're shown a live account at @targethandle with good stats. You pay. The "transfer" is done — but when you check, the handle you now have is @targethandle_ or @target.handle (with underscores or dots you didn't notice). The original handle was never part of the transaction; the scammer simply changed their own account to a slight variation.

Always verify the exact handle — character by character — at the moment of transfer on the live call.

## Red Flag #6: Paid Verification or "Account Restoration" Promises

Some scammers offer handles that are "temporarily suspended" and promise to get them restored for an additional fee. Or they claim a verified account can be transferred if you pay a "verification transfer fee." Neither of these is real. Suspended accounts cannot be un-suspended by a third party for a fee. Verified (blue checkmark) status cannot be transferred — it is tied to the specific identity that was verified.

If a handle is currently suspended, walk away.

## Red Flag #7: No Verifiable Broker Identity

A legitimate broker has a name, a location, and a contact trail. Their Telegram username is consistent across mentions. Their business details can be independently verified. If the person you're dealing with has no verifiable identity — anonymous Telegram account, no business address, no consistent online presence — you have no recourse if the deal goes wrong.

Ask any broker for their full name, location, and business registration details. A broker who refuses to provide this has something to hide.
